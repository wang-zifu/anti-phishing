Our long-held view of Aereo’s legality was clearly wrong in the face of yesterday’s 6-3 Supreme Court decision.  We believed, in keeping with the dissent’s view (written by Justice Scalia), that Aereo was legally-exploiting a loophole and that broadcasters should have been seeking legislative rather than judicial relief.  Instead, the majority’s view, written by Justice Breyer, chose to determine that Aereo violated copyright law by viewing it within the cable system fra mework established by the 1976 Copyright Act.  The ’76 Act redefined what a cable operator was in overturning Supreme Court rulings in both the Fortnightly and Teleprompter cases.

New IP-Based Class of Cable Operators?

The Supreme Court’s rationale in finding Aereo to be illegal opened up a new question: Are domain.com-based retransmission services now cable operators in the eyes of copyright law?  Historically, IP-based retransmitters have not been eligible to be cable operators.  Yet, when you read yesterday’s majority opinion it is very difficult to not come away believing that a meaningful change has occurred in the legal definition of a cable company...
